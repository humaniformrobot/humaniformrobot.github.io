Medical Tourism

Meet Sarah, a hypothetical 42-year-old graphic designer from Seattle with chronic knee pain. After years of suffering, her doctor recommended a total knee replacement. The quote from her local hospital: $45,000, with her insurance covering only 70%. Sarah faced a $13,500 out-of-pocket expense she couldn't afford.

Desperate for options, Sarah joined a Facebook group for knee pain sufferers where someone mentioned medical tourism. After extensive research, she connected with a JCI-accredited hospital in Thailand specializing in orthopedic procedures. Their all-inclusive package: $10,000 for the surgery, a two-week recovery in a private room, physical therapy, and follow-up care.

Sarah used her savings to book the procedure and a three-week trip to Thailand. The surgery was performed by a surgeon who had trained at Johns Hopkins. Her recovery room overlooked a tropical garden, and her physical therapist visited twice daily. When she returned home, she was pain-free for the first time in years and had even managed to visit some temples during her final recovery week.

Total cost including airfare, surgery, accommodation, and even some sightseeing: $13,000 - still less than her out-of-pocket costs would have been at home.

More Than Just Bargain Hunting

This story isn't just a clever travel hack or a goofy anecdote. It's a part of a multi-billion dollar industry that raises many ethical questions.

Examining Medical tourism sheds light on global inequities in income, healthcare access, healthcare cost, and heathcare quality.

When millions of people need to cross international borders for affordable care, something is fundamentally broken in how we structure healthcare systems. Medical tourism shows the bizarre contradictions in our global healthcare landscape. World-class care can exist just miles from abject medical poverty, a procedure might cost 10x more on one side of a border than the other, and your passport can determine your health outcomes.

What is Medical Tourism?

Medical tourism refers to people traveling for medical care for many different reasons

  • Significantly Cheaper
  • Has shorter or no wait times
  • Better quality
  • The only option for certain procedures

A Brief History of Medical Tourism

Medical tourism isn't new. Medical Tourism has been going on since ancient times. Throughout history, if you were dying or suffering, you might travel any distance for the hope of a cure if you could afford it.

Ancient Greece

As early as 500 BC, Ancient Greeks traveled significant distances to sanctuaries dedicated to the demigod Asklepios. Aelius Aristides was one patient of such a sanctuary in the second century A.D. While there he followed treatments including exercising, bathing in cold water, and eating and avoiding certain foods. Pilgrims might also receive herbs or medicine, bathe in thermal springs, and participate in spiritually significant rituals.

These healing sanctuaries were marked by the serpent staff symbol, which is a symbol of medicine to this day.

Victorian Spa Tourism

During Victorian times, medical tourism was a popular trend among the wealthy who traveled as an attempt to improve their physical and mental health. People sought destinations known for their healing environments, such as mineral springs, clean air, or warm climates.

Bath, England, became a popular destination for medical tourists due to its hot springs. Visitors believed the waters had healing powers and came for massages, vapor baths, and other therapies. The Swiss Alps also became popular, especially for patients with respiratory diseases like tuberculosis, who benefited from the fresh mountain air.

Travelers often went to specific destinations for particular ailments. Warmer places like Italy, Egypt, and the French Riviera helped people with respiratory issues, while spas in Germany and England offered treatments for nervous conditions. Some travelers also sought alternative medicine such as Ayurveda, acupuncture, and herbal remedies from places like India, China, and Sri Lanka.

Health resorts played a key role by providing both medical and leisure services. These retreats allowed patients to rest in peaceful, scenic environments while receiving treatments. Medical tourism was largely limited to the wealthy due to the high cost of travel and care.

Much of the popularity of medical tourism at this time was due to the lack of understanding of medicine.

Modern Medical Tourism

As biomedicine's understanding of how to fix certain problems has improved, this idea of using exotic changes of scenery to try to cure yourself of an ailment has not left the minds of many people, and the health benefits of a getaway are still touted by companies marketing normal vacations.

The modern medical tourism industry as we know it today really took off in the 1990s and 2000s. This was driven by several factors:

  • Skyrocketing healthcare costs in countries like the US
  • Improved international accreditation for hospitals
  • The internet making it easier to research foreign providers
  • Budget airlines making international travel more affordable
  • Growing middle classes in many countries who wanted better care than was available locally

By the 2010s, entire hospital complexes in places like Thailand, India, and Mexico were being built specifically to cater to international patients. Countries like Singapore and Malaysia began actively promoting themselves as medical tourism destinations, complete with government support and special medical visitor visas.

Today, medical tourism is a multi-billion dollar industry. In 2024 millions of patients traveled internationally for treatment, generating over $40 billion in revenue. [1]

$8778.73 billion is the total size of the world healthcare market,[2] so medical tourism is around half a percent of total. For a more tangable number, if you spent $10,000 a year on healthcare, medical tourism would only be $50, so medical tourism is a small component of global healthcare spending.

Medical tourism isn't new – it's just been rebranded and enlarged. People have always traveled for health reasons; we've just replaced sacred healing springs with JCI-accredited hospitals and Instagram-worthy recovery resorts.

Better Quality than at home

You might think that medical tourism means sacrificing quality for cost, but that's often not the case. In fact, some destinations offer care that equals or exceeds what's available in supposedly "advanced" healthcare systems.

What Makes Quality Care?

  • International accreditation: Many foreign hospitals have Joint Commission International (JCI) certification – the same standard used in the US
  • Physician training: Many doctors at top international hospitals trained at prestigious Western medical schools
  • Specialization: Some facilities perform certain procedures at much higher volumes than Western hospitals, leading to better outcomes
  • Patient experience: Private rooms, better staff-to-patient ratios, and more personalized care

Bumrungrad International Hospital in Bangkok treats over a million patients annually, with 520,000 international patients from 190 countries. It has more US board-certified doctors than any other hospital outside the United States. In places, it looks more like a luxury hotel than a hospital with concierge services, interpreters for over 20 languages, and airport pickup.

[4]

Procedures Unavailable or Restricted

Sometimes it's not about cost – it's about access. Many people travel because certain procedures are unavailable, illegal, or have extremely long waiting lists in their home countries.

Organ Transplants

Waiting lists for organs in countries like the US, UK, and Canada can be years long. Some patients travel to countries with different allocation systems or living donor programs.

[5]

Fertility Treatments

Many countries have restrictions on certain fertility procedures, age limits, or don't cover them under national health plans. Popular destinations to recieve treatment include Spain, Czech Republic, and Greece, where costs are lower.

[6]

Gender-Affirming Care

With increasing restrictions in some regions, transgender patients often travel for surgeries and treatments. Thailand has become particularly known for gender affirmation surgeries, with specialized clinics and experienced surgeons.

Does Crossing State Lines Count?

With the overturning of Roe v. Wade in the US, many Americans now travel to other states for abortion access. This might be considered medical tourism. It fits a possible definition of medical tourism of people traveling outside their home region for healthcare unavailable locally.

What Is Medical Tourism?

Before we go further, let's take a step back and ask: what exactly counts as medical tourism? The boundaries get blurry fast.

Is it medical tourism if...

  • You drive three hours to see a specialist in another state?
  • You use telemedicine to consult with a doctor in another country?
  • You order prescription medications from an online Canadian pharmacy?
  • You're an expat who returns to your home country for medical treatment?
  • You're injured while on vacation and receive emergency care?

Medical tourism exists on a spectrum. At one end, there's a planned international trip specifically for a given procedure. At the other, there's the reality that there are deep regional healthcare inequities, and people often need to travel (whether across town or across oceans) to get the care they need. Medical tourism isn't some exotic outlier, it's just a tail end of a distribution of the normal human experience of navigating unfair healthcare systems. I personally think that medical tourism is when you go across country borders for the explicit purpose of receiving healthcare, but the definition is not exact.

Is This Ethical?

Now we get to the hard part. Medical tourism raises a lot ethical questions that don't have easy answers.

Climate Costs

Let's start with the obvious: flying halfway around the world for healthcare has a significant carbon footprint. A round-trip flight from New York to Bangkok generates around 1 metric ton of CO2 per passenger (about a fourth of what a passenger car would emit in a year.) It is not particularly energy efficient to ferry people halfway around the world for medical services.

But this raises a question: is it fair to ask individuals to sacrifice potentially life-changing or life-saving care for climate reasons when the healthcare systems in their home countries have failed them? Should the burden of climate responsibility fall on patients seeking affordable care, or on the systems that made that care inaccessible in the first place?

Inequality and Exploitation

Medical tourism can create or reinforce troubling inequalities in destination countries. When hospitals prioritize wealthy international patients, it can divert resources away from local healthcare needs.

There's also the question of labor exploitation. Medical professionals in destination countries often earn a fraction of what their counterparts make in wealthy nations, despite comparable training and skills. This wage gap is part of what makes medical tourism possible – but is it fair?

In some countries, you can find world-class private hospitals catering to international patients right next to underfunded public facilities struggling to provide basic care to locals. This creates a two-tier system where quality care becomes a luxury good available primarily to foreigners and wealthy locals.

Are we outsourcing our health privilege? When Americans or Europeans travel to Thailand or Mexico for affordable care, are we essentially benefiting from global inequality by getting high-quality care at prices only possible because of economic disparities arising from western exploitation?

Masking Problems in Local Systems

Medical tourism can function as a pressure release valve for dysfunctional healthcare systems. When people who can afford to travel go elsewhere for care, it reduces the political pressure to fix problems at home.

In the United States, medical costs are astronomical and tens of millions remain uninsured or underinsured.

[8]

When middle-class Americans can escape the worst consequences of this system by traveling abroad for care, does that make comprehensive healthcare reform less likely?

In countries with national health services and long waiting lists (like Canada or the UK), medical tourism is used by many who don't want to wait. The option of private care abroad might make it easier for governments to underfund public systems, when some of those motivated to make change simply leave.

[9]

Is this actually bad?

Medical tourism provides immediate relief to real people with urgent needs. While we wait for perfect healthcare systems (which may never arrive), medical tourism offers a solution for those who can access it. Is it fair to ask individuals who are able to be healthy to sacrifice their health for the sake of potential systemic change?

Where Does the Money Go?

Medical tourism brings significant revenue to destination countries. For example in 2023 Thailand received $2.57 billion in revenue from medical tourism. In 2024, the Philipines recieved $3.28 billion in revenue.

[10][11]

This creates jobs, and can potentially improve healthcare capacity in destination countries at little cost to the healthcare system. In Thailand, medical tourism accounts for 10% of tourism revenue while having neglible impacts on the healthcare system.

[12]

When Medical Tourism Goes Wrong

Not all medical tourism experiences end well. While reputable facilities offer excellent care, the industry also has its share of horror stories.

  • Botched procedures: Particularly common with cosmetic surgeries at unaccredited facilities
  • Antibiotic-resistant infections: Some regions have higher rates of drug-resistant bacteria and a lack of access to alternate antibiotics.
  • Inadequate follow-up care: Patients return home and local doctors may be reluctant to treat complications
  • Insurance refusals: Many insurers won't cover complications from overseas procedures
  • Fraudulent credentials: Some facilities misrepresent their accreditation or physician qualifications

In 2019, at least 6 American patients contracted severe infections after weight-loss surgeries at Grand View Hospital in Tijuana, Mexico. The CDC identified the bacteria as Pseudomonas aeruginosa, a drug-resistant strain rarely seen in the US. The hospital failed to follow proper sterilization procedures, and Mexican officials closed it down for a time until it began to follow proper procedures.

[13]

Final Thoughts

Medical tourism exists in a strange gray area. It simultaneously represents:

  • A practical solution for individuals failed by their local healthcare systems
  • A symptom of profound global healthcare inequality
  • A potential driver of healthcare innovation and cost competition
  • A possible obstacle to systemic healthcare reform

Medical tourism is a kind of mirror. It reflects back the contradictions and inequities in our global healthcare landscape. When a hip replacement costs $50,000 in Boston but $10,000 in Bangkok, something is deeply wrong.

Medical tourism also challenges our assumptions about where "good healthcare" exists. The notion that the best care is always found in wealthy Western nations is increasingly outdated. Excellence exists everywhere, often at a fraction of the cost found in supposedly "developed" systems.

In an ideal world, medical tourism as we know it wouldn't exist. People wouldn't need to fly thousands of miles for affordable care. Quality treatment would be available locally, and healthcare wouldn't bankrupt families.

But until that world arrives, millions will continue crossing borders in search of care they can't find or afford at home.
